The Qualitative Approach

On the other hand, qualitative approaches are most commonly used when a researcher seeks to answer a "what," "how," or "why" question. Instead of numbers, qualitative researchers rely on descriptions of various social interactions between people. These might include interviews, observing group conversations, or observation. (We'll describe common data gathering tools for both quantitative and qualitative approaches in Module 6: Data Collection). One interesting qualitative technique might even involve asking participants to take photographs of things they feel are important, as defined by the researcher, and then analysing those photographs.

The purpose of qualitative research is to offer a rich "portrait" of a moment in time in order to better understand social contexts. This approach is best suited for complex social environments where multiple factors might be at play simultaneously. While quantitative research could tell us how many women are working in the highest echelons of the television industry as compared to their male counterparts, for example, qualitative research might help us to understand why there is a difference. The promotion of women over men is an example of a social phenomenon that is highly complex and not easily understood with methods that simply quantify or "count."

While quantitative researchers rarely meet their participants (the sheer number of them often precludes a face-to-face encounter), qualitative researchers often do. For this reason, qualitative research typically recruits fewer participants even fewer than 20 participants can produce adequate data for qualitative research. Additionally, qualitative researchers must carefully manage their own involvement in the project; good qualitative researchers try to identify possible biases at the outset of the project and constantly evaluate the ways they may be influencing the data collected. Are they simply finding what they expect to find? And if so, how can those expectations be managed so that the data truly speaks for itself?

Dusit/Shutterstock.com

While every step of the research process presents challenges (many researchers would say enjoyable challenges), some point out that quantitative research requires real effort at the beginning, during the design phase, and qualitative research calls for a big effort near the end, during data analysis. Qualitative researchers often wind up with tons of data through which they must carefully sort, coding for themes and interpreting them. You will learn a little more about the data analysis required for both quantitative and qualitative data in Module 7: Data Analysis.

Just like quantitative research, qualitative research has certain identifiable limitations. For one thing, findings can be difficult to generalize. Owing to the fact that qualitative research assumes that each social context is unique and complex, the conclusions derived from one study may not easily apply to another social environment. Qualitative data may also take a long time to collect and analyse.
