Not all research is reliable research. Good researchers demonstrate objectivity: this means that they attempt to mitigate their pre-conceived inclination to a certain set of findings by allowing the data to speak for itself. Careful researchers take steps to ensure repeatability: that given precisely the same circumstances, the same findings would likely manifest again and again. Additionally, all human-centered research carried out at Humber College (and elsewhere) must meet strict ethical standards ensuring the safety of both researchers and participants. In these ways, the research projects you will encounter will probably look very similar. This doesn't mean, however, that all research conducted at Humber College is exactly the same.

Research projects differ in many ways. For example, the time and resources available may affect the design of the project. A researcher that sets out to chart the future earnings of current Humber graduates may require many years to collect data. On the other hand, another researcher seeking to learn reasons that Humber students did or did not vote in the last SFU election can collect data within just one or two weeks. Some projects secure generous funding and can therefore hire more assistants and rent expensive equipment or space; others are one-man or one-woman shows. The resources and time available may determine some of the many differences between research projects.

Issues of accessibility might also shape differences between research projects. A researcher examining homeless people in Toronto may need to secure access to shelters, while another might choose to pound the pavement searching for willing participants. Some researchers are granted access to privileged documents or have personal contacts or experiences that support their research focus. Issues of accessibility can either limit or liberate a researcher's decisions when it comes to selecting the best research plan.
