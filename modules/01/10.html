The Quantitative Approach

Creativa Images/Shutterstock.com

Quantitative research is typically used to answers questions related to quantity, like how much? or how many? or to what proportion? In other words, quantitative research counts things. For this reason, if a researcher seeks to understand large-scale social processes or trends, quantitative research is usually the preferred approach. Quantitative research generates findings indicating things like how many common-law couples are living in Ontario, how many women are using food banks, and how much richer are families in certain neighbourhoods compared to others.

Quantitative researchers use research tools like surveys with yes or no answers or scales from 1 to 5 or 1 to 10. Because quantitative researchers count things, they might, for example, count the number of times a word is repeated in a newspaper article or even how many times an object or person appears in news photos.

Take a look at this article from the Toronto Star featuring an example of quantitative research:

This kind of research examines relatively narrow social processes that the researcher feels can be measured measurements that the researcher firmly believes can and will tell us something interesting and important about the world.

The advantages of quantitative research are that numbers can be delightfully precise. They are easily understood. They can be tested and retested. As such, they are conveniently, confidently, and easily shared. Important policy decisions are often made with the support of quantitative research owing to its reliability.

Many feel that quantitative research mitigates the potential bias of the researcher by depersonalizing the data collection. In other words, unlike in an interview setting, participants cannot be influenced by the researcher's head nodding in agreement when they check off a yes-no box on a survey. Likewise, the researcher's particular inclination towards certain findings are not as likely to blind them to certain kinds of data, which, as you will discover, can be a limitation in other forms of research.

Quantitative research does, however, have some limitations. For one thing, quantitative research can be based on a belief that social reality is relatively stable and unchanging. People are unpredictable, and reducing social interactions to a set of numbers may not produce a complete picture. Quantitative research can count how many times a person does something, but it is not a research strategy that tells us much about why.

Sometimes, quantitative measurements require the production of an artificial environment (for example, testing someone's stress reactions in a laboratory setting). These conditions may be so artificial that they yield findings that are not applicable in real world settings.

Quantitative research will show that university-educated women are twice as likely as high-school-educated women to marry outside their own ethnic group. However, only qualitative research can help us understand how the women made the marriage choices they did: why these women were attracted to their spouses and how they overcame their and their families' prejudices.

(Northey, Tepperman, & Albanese, 2009)
