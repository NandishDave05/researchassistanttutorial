Mixed Methods

LeventeGyori/Shutterstock.com

Both quantitative and qualitative approaches are incredibly valuable to research in the social sciences. In spite of their differences, often the two approaches can be beautifully combined in a research project. You might be lucky enough to be engaged in a project that attempts and achieves exactly that!

One common way to mix the approaches is in survey design. Because quantitative surveys often require closed-ended¬ù (yes or no) questions, scales, or limited options, some researchers may find once their data has been collected that the questions they asked were unclear or limiting. For example, consider a survey that asks Humber students to indicate which stress relieving activity - from a list of five options - they prefer. How will the survey designer compose this list? What if an important stress reliever like playing video games, for example, is absent from the list? The data collected will be incomplete.

A series of pre-survey focus groups, a method sometimes referred to as an "exploratory qualitative" component, might encourage students to speak freely about their stress relieving choices. The preferences that turn up in those settings can then be used as a basis for the limited options on a survey.
