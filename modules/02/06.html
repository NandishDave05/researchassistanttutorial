RESEARCH ASSISTANT

Legal and Business Considerations

Intellectual Property Cont’d

When working on research projects, you are likely to come across several key concepts and terms related to IP with which you should be familiar:

Creation / Invention

Creation / Invention: an original work (e.g. song, novel, painting, software, machine, formula) created through a creative endeavor. Creations / inventions are protected by laws related to the six different types of IP, listed above.

Ownership

Ownership: the act, state or right of possessing something (physical or intellectual)

Copyright

Copyright: the exclusive legal right to produce, reproduce, publish or perform an original literary, artistic, dramatic or musical work. The creator is usually the copyright owner. However, an employer—for example, a film studio—may have copyright in works created by employees unless there is an agreement in place stating otherwise.

Moral Rights

Moral rights: the rights that the author of a work has:

  1. to the integrity of the work;
  2. where reasonable in the circumstances, to be associated with the work as its author by name or under a pseudonym and the right to remain anonymous. While copyright can be defined as an economic right, moral rights are to protect the honour and reputation of an author. While copyright in a work may be assigned, the moral rights remain with the author, and cannot be assigned, but only waived, which means surrendered or given up. Humber students normally retain the moral rights to the project they work on.

Patents

Patents: License that confers a right or title for a set period, especially the sole right to exclude others from making, using or selling an invention.2 Patent protection applies in the country or region that issues it. The inventor that files a patent must provide a full description of the invention. This helps enrich technical knowledge worldwide. Details of patent applications filed in Canada are disclosed to the public after an 18-month period of confidentiality.

To be eligible for patent protection, the invention must be:

  • new—first in the world
  • useful—functional and operative
  • inventive—showing ingenuity and not obvious to someone of average skill who works in the field of the invention

Public Domain

Public Domain: IP that is not owned or controlled by anyone. Materials in the Public Domain are available for anyone to use for any purpose since they belong to the public. Works in the public domain can be used free of charge and do not require written permission from the author/creator. Works can be in the public domain for a variety of reasons. Examples: the term of the copyright has expired, the work was not eligible for copyright protection in the first place, or the copyright owner has authorized the public to use the work without permission or payment.

Assignment

Assignment: The transfer of some or all of IP rights to another party.

Royalties

Royalties: Payments to the owner for use of property, in this context: patents or copyrighted work

Infringement

Infringement: breach or violation of the rules in the copyright law.

Background IP

Background IP: All IP first conceived, developed or produced prior to the commencement of the project.

Foreground IP

Foreground IP: All IP first conceived, developed or produced as part of the project.

A major consideration when talking about IP is commercialization – or the financial gain from selling a product or service on the market.

Typically, colleges do not seek to own IP created as a result of research – the legal default agreement between Humber and its research partner organizations is that IP is the property of the industry or community partner. The partner then has to manage the various aspects of IP related to commercialization: revenues and profits from sales, the transfer of (re)assignment of the IP to another party, distribution of any profits, and any associated legal processes.
