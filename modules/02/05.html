Intellectual Property Cont’d

So, why is IP important?

In a free-market capitalist economy, few things provide an enterprise with a lasting competitive advantage – or attributes that allow a business to outperform its competitors. IP is one of them. It can result in a monetizeable asset that, in turn, can lead to a long-term, exclusive, statutory (legal) protection.

 

When you watch Dragon’s Den, one consideration for the Dragons who are valuing a business and deciding whether to invest in it is whether the entrepreneurs have suitable IP, such as patents, that can keep competitors away. In an increasingly knowledge-based economy, IP is valued even more.

So, what kinds of intellectual property are there?

We have six statutory forms of intellectual property in Canada. These are trademarks, copyrights, patents, industrial designs, plant breeder’s rights, and integrated circuit (IC) topographies. Other countries or jurisdictions may have more or fewer forms.

In Canada, IP is governed by federal laws. Sometimes, within contracts, parties may define IP more broadly, to include proprietary information, trade secrets, etc. Lately, courts are treating domain names as IP, as extensions of trademarks. We can also imagine social media handles (e.g. Twitter handles) being considered the same way too. Geographical indicators such as Scotch whiskey or Basmati rice are considered IP too.

Some examples of IP created on (applied) research projects include prototypes of machines or machine parts, chemical formulae, photographs and other media output.
