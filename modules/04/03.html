Creating A Work Plan

Researchers often know where they want to wind up (with more knowledge about a particular subject) and the steps they must take to get there (the research process). Coming up with a strategy for achieving each step in the research process is called creating a work plan.

Creating a work plan requires that a researcher break the work into manageable chunks, a process that has been called decomposition (much like what happens in a compost heap). If you are assisting in a research project, you may note that the supervising researcher has decomposed the project and sectioned off pieces to delegate to you (and maybe others too, depending on the size and scope of the project). Once your research supervisor has assigned you your tasks, you will also need to sit down by yourself and budget the time and resources you will need to accomplish your tasks.

Source: Aila Images/Shutterstock.com

For example, your research supervisor might assign you the task of recruiting and interviewing participants at a job fair. This sounds like a pretty straightforward job, but it actually contains seven (or more) sub-tasks that you will need to complete under the guidance of your supervisor:

  1. Secure permission from the job fair hosts
  2. Prepare your interview tools and materials (i.e., question guide, paper for notes or recording device, pens for participants to sign documents, professional attire)
  3. Compose and photocopy informed consent forms
  4. Prepare the method of recruitment; will you have an information board, pamphlet, or poster ready for people to read more about your project? How will people be sure you are who you say you are? Will you need to bring ID or wear a nametag?
  5. Prepare your delivery: what will you say to potential participants to engage and inform them?
  6. Develop a method of safely storing your notes during and after your data collection
  7. Transporting yourself to and from the job fair in a timely fashion
