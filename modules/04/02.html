Project Management

What is a "Project"?

Source: SFIO CRACHO/Shutterstock.com

Often, you will hear the word “project” to describe the kind of work you will be doing as a research assistant. Research projects can be large or small, short or lengthy. They can include a great number of people or only one. But what makes a project a project? Here are some characteristics typical of projects from Stanley Portny’s Project Management for Dummies (2013).

A Project...

  • ...Is Typically Temporary
  • ...Has Specific Goals
  • ...Uses Specific Resources

First, a project is typically temporary. Well-designed projects have specific timelines and schedules; that means, at some point the project will end, unlike a process, which is a series of steps required to perform a function again and again.

A project also has specific goals. In the case of a research project, the goals are typically to test a theory and discover something new. The results of research projects are often used to advance knowledge in a particular field, contributing to policy development, service delivery, and general societal wellbeing.

Finally, projects use specific resources. This may include people, equipment, time, etc. Ideally, the resources required to complete a project are known in advance. Acquiring the necessary supplies for a project is part of what researchers call creating a work plan.
