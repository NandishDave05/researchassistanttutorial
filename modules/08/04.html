Report Writing

Summarizing and Interpreting the Data

Source: Dean Drobot/Shutterstock.com

Once the processes of data analysis have been described, reports should summarize and interpret the data.

Summarizing the data means drawing out and underlining the important findings. For example, consider a qualitative research project that seeks to understand the reasons why women use homeless shelters less than men. After coding interview transcripts, the researcher might discover that her findings support other research into this topic. These findings are important to mention because they add strength and credibility to the existing knowledge in the field. However, the researcher might devote comparably more time and space in her report to the any finding that adds unique, new understandings in order to expand knowledge in the field.

Interpreting the data involves identifying the significance and implications of the findings. For example, consider a quantitative data set that shows increased use of Humber’s Math Centre has more of a positive effect on academic achievement than other math learning aids like group work, solo studying, or online videos.

This finding is significant when it is not likely to have resulted from chance alone, and it is up to the researchers to prove that. The data can also show degrees of significance.

Most research reports contain implicit or explicit recommendations for behaviour. This is sometimes referred to as the research’s implications. Recall our Math Centre example: the implication of this finding is that, in the future, resources (like the students’ time, or Humber College funding) might be most efficiently directed at the Math Centre as opposed to other types learning aids.

"The impact of social norms and personal values on the way we interpret events pretty well guarantees that, to a greater or lesser extent, any account of research should be regarded as a version of the truth rather than a literal depiction of what happened. Within the social sciences, the idea of a purely objective position is controversial, and a researcher would be naïve to presume that his or her account can stand, without careful consideration, as an "objective description of what really occurred."

(Denscombe, 2010)
