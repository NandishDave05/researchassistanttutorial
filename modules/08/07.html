Report Writing

Different Types of Presentations

The research reports you assist with may wind up in any number of places. In this section, we’ll go over some of the most common destinations for the work: peer reviewed journals, poster/PowerPoint presentations, and internal reports.

Peer Reviewed Journals

Source: Dean Drobot/Shutterstock.com

A peer-reviewed journal is a periodical (meaning, it is published at set intervals of time, maybe 1, 2, or even 6 times per year) intended for mostly academic audiences. Journals are organized according to theme, and the articles selected for inclusion are written and reviewed by experts in the field – that is where the term "peer reviewed" comes from. They are considered highly credible sources of information, albeit sometimes slightly out dated as the revision process can take a few months or more.

Articles submitted to peer reviewed journals tend to follow a predictable structure. It looks something like this.

The introduction to a peer-reviewed article typically contains the title, abstract, key words, list of contents, list of tables of figures, preface, acknowledgements, and list of abbreviations.

The main text of the article typically contains the introduction, a literature review, the methods of investigation, the findings, the discussion and analysis, and conclusions and recommendations.

Finally, a journal article typically ends with the appendices, the notes, the references, and sometimes an index.

The general assumption is that peer reviewed journal articles will be read by others in your field of research. For that reason, the vocabulary is typically high-level and academic, partly because there is an assumption of shared understanding and expertise. However, this can be frustrating for students who are new to the field and must dive right into the deep end!
