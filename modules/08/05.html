Differences Between Qualitative and Quantitative Research Reports

Source: Dean Drobot/Shutterstock.com

Research reports sometimes differ in their presentation depending on the research approach used in the project. For one thing, quantitative data and findings lend themselves well to tables and charts. Readers can easily compare values and appreciate change in numbers when presented in such visual formats.

On the other hand, qualitative research typically includes a fairly large volume of words or even images that can be difficult — yet not impossible — to depict visually. Many qualitative researchers must decide which of many interview excerpts to feature in a final report — including all would be impossible. For this reason, it is typically understood that qualitative researchers play an "editorial" role when presenting data in their reports. The choice of which interview excerpts to show — and which to leave behind — is fully dependent on the writerâ€™s discretion. As such, excerpts may act as examples to support conclusions instead of evidence to prove them.

Another thing to keep in mind when selecting and presenting interview excerpts is that excerpts are presented out of context. Denscombe cautions that when taken out of context, "the meaning of the words is changed by the fact that they are not linked to what came before and what was said after" (2010). Smart researchers will carefully select excerpts that run a low risk of being misinterpreted on these grounds. This can be achieved by providing some indication of the context of the conversation and/or by offering some background on the person speaking.
