Sampling Continued

It is often helpful to imagine sampling just like making a delicious stew: if you wanted to taste your stew to see if it needed a little more spice, you probably would not simply skim a watery spoonful off the top. You would likely give your stew a good stir to ensure all the bits of veggies, meat, and spices have a chance to wind up on your sampling spoon. You want your sample taste to be representative of the stew to give you the best idea of what is happening, taste-wise, in that bubbling pot. Likewise, good researchers want to ensure that their sample features the variety inherent in the target population.

Sometimes, a representative sample is achieved through sheer random selection, otherwise known as probability sampling. This approach is informed by a theory that a random selection will offer a normal distribution of difference among participants. In other words, if you roll a dice ten times, you are likely to hit on a nice representation of most of the numbers on the dice.

It is critical that in a random sample, all members of your target population have an equal chance to wind up in your sample. Say, for example, a researcher wished to recruit a random sample of Humber students and only approached potential participants in the F building (the “culinary wing” of campus). Those students in the nursing program would not have the same chances to participate in the study, and therefore, this researcher’s “random” sample would not be random enough (thus, not representative).

Another approach to generating a representative sample is to deliberately set out to recruit certain kinds of people. This approach is known as non-probability sampling. If your research project focusing on Humber students used non-probability sampling, you might deliberately recruit students from each program and an equal number of men and women to participate in your study. Some researchers feel non-probability is the best way to guarantee a representative sample.

If you are asked to help conduct large-scale quantitative research, you will be responsible for helping your research supervisor ensure that his or her sample is representative. This means that if you are recruiting volunteer participants, you must be sure to approach a good variety of people within the demographic perimeters established by your supervisor.
