Sample Size

Imagine you opened a fresh bag of chips – and imagine chips are your very favourite snack. Imagine you have been on a special diet and you have been waiting a month for this nice bag of chips. And then…you crunch into the first chip only to discover it has gone stale.

How many more chips would you eat before concluding the entire bag is contaminated? One more? Two more?

This is a very unscientific example of the accuracy of sample size. Consider the example of a research project involving Humber students. If a researcher wishes to learn something about all Humber students, asking ten to participate in a survey, and drawing conclusions based on those ten responses, would be invalid. Asking every single one of the approximate seventy-five thousand Humber students would be unnecessary. So, the researcher has to come up with a number between ten and seventy-five thousand. What’s the magic number?

Thankfully, we have some mathematics on our side. Statisticians have developed formulas that help researchers develop targets for reasonable sample populations. These formulas generate sample sizes that offer high degrees of accuracy. Additionally, “there is relatively little advantage to be gained in terms of accuracy once a sample has reached a given size” (Denscombe, 2010), so abiding by these formulas prevents researchers from doing unnecessary extra work.

These formulas also indicate margin of error. What is a margin of error? Simply put, a margin of error represents the confidence level of a finding – the degree to which it can be trusted. For example, a survey might have a margin of error of 5%. This typically means that if the survey were repeated 100 times, almost every time the data would be within 5% above or 5% below the current percentage reflected in the findings.

Here are some concrete examples of the kind of numbers we are talking about: a sample size of 9,513 participants is recommended for a target population of 1 million and a margin of error of 1%. If a researcher can live with a larger margin of error of, say 5%, she would only need a sample size of 384 participants.

A sample size of 3,288 participants is recommended for a population of 5,000 with a margin of error of 1%. Or, with a margin of error of 5%, researchers can safely recruit a sample population of only 357. Often, researchers will sacrifice a few percent in the margin of error in order to decrease the size of their sample population (and therefore, their work load).

Many researchers turn to these helpful statistical guides before setting out to recruit a representative sample population.
