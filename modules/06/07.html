Data Collection

Qualitative Data Collection Methods

If quantitative data can be said to be in the form of numbers, qualitative data is generally collected in the form of words. Qualitative data can be collected using a variety of tools.

  • Surveys
  • Interviews
  • Focus Groups
  • Observation

Surveys

Source: FabrikaSimf/Shutterstock.com

Surveys (sometimes called questionnaires) are also a common tool for qualitative research. Qualitative researchers design their surveys in a particular way, a way that often looks a lot different than quantitative surveys.

For example, qualitative surveys often use open-ended questions. There are questions that elicit longer, more complex answers. Some examples of open-ended questions might be:

  • What are your most valued possessions?
  • How would you describe your last argument with your husband?
  • Which are the most important steps in planning a vacation?

The selection of answers is not provided in a qualitative survey. The respondent is encouraged to write whatever and however much he or she feels is necessary to respond to the question. Typically, qualitative researchers are delighted to receive big, juicy answers when they collect their surveys because it may indicate there is lots of data to pour through.

Interviews

Another extremely common method for collecting qualitative data is to interview participants. Interviewing is a skill many qualitative researchers take years to perfect. It is not easy, and it takes practice. The following are explanations of valuable interviewing skills.

Flexibility

A single interviewer has to think about a lot of things at the same time. For one thing, in a semi-structured interview, a participant may jump ahead and unknowingly answer a question the interviewer had prepared for later in the interview. The interviewer must adapt and adjust according to the responses of the participant, following him or her down tangential thoughts if the interviewer feels they may be valuable directions to go.

Note Taking Skills

Additionally, the interviewer must record careful notes. This is why some interviewers opt to digitally record interviews and transcribe them later; it can be a challenge to take notes and listen at the same time. Others choose to do a team interview if resources permit. This means inviting another researcher along who will be in charge of documenting the participants' responses. Very few researchers can spare the extra hands for team interviews, however.

Communication Skills

Words are not the only way that humans communicate. Interviews also involve tone, body language, and eye contact. Researchers must develop methods to track and document relevant aspects of non-verbal communication in their participants. Likewise, they must practice an open, welcoming, and non-judgemental approach to facilitate comfortable communication. It is difficult to collect reliable data from uncomfortable participants who feel they are being pressured into speaking.

Sensitivity

Not all participants tell the truth. Lying (or truth-bending) can happen in any type of human research. Good researchers, however, are especially sensitive to false responses when interviewing and may save the questions that are likely to embarrass for the written surveys. They know that it is easier to truthfully answer uncomfortable or embarrassing questions anonymously; it is less easy when someone is looking you in the eye!

Mixed Use

Did you know qualitative interviews can be a source of quantitative data too? Some quantitative researchers can pour through interview transcripts and do what is called a content analysis. That typically means that they count the number of times a word, phrase, or sentence is used. For example, a quantitative researcher might go through all the interviews conducted with Rob Ford in the 2010 Toronto mayoral election and note all the times the phrase gravy train was used. The use of this catchphrase compared to others might tell us something about campaign messaging priorities.
