Data Collection

Focus Groups

Source: wavebreakmedia/Shutterstock.com

Focus groups are another method of gathering qualitative data. This is a particularly popular strategy in marketing research, but they are used in many, many other settings as well.

Focus groups require a small group of participants gathered in a room together with a moderator, who acts on behalf of the researcher (in some cases, when appropriate, the moderator is the researcher).

Once informed consent has been granted, the moderator begins the group discussion around a topic related to the research, and the participants chat away. The advantage of focus groups is that the data may build data: the discussion contribution by one participant may spark a response from another. This is sometimes called “sharing and comparing” and may garner more data than interviews alone. The moderator is there to gently guide the group discussion in a relevant, useful direction. Careful note taking is a must, of course, so some moderators ask the participants to consent to audio or video documentation, or a second researcher is on hand to record notes.

There are special confidentiality concerns when using focus groups as a method of gathering qualitative data. For one thing, it is impossible to guarantee anonymity; participants are staring right at each other! So, researchers typically ensure that participants are aware of the confidentiality issues and encourage participants to be discreet about what is shared during the group discussion. Promoting an atmosphere of trust is critical to open conversation. An atmosphere of trust not only results in a more pleasant experience for all involved, it will also garner better data.
