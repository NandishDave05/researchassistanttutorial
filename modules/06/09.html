RESEARCH ASSISTANT

Data Collection

The 3 P's of Observation

Some qualitative researchers gather data through observation. Unlike the other forms of qualitative data collection featured previously, observation does not generate data about what people say they do or say they think. Instead, observation gathers data about what people actually do.

Perception

Source: Algonga/Shutterstock.com

Observation typically occurs in real-life settings (not in a laboratory). The researcher must be highly aware of the fact that observation is not an objective act; what a person sees is often contingent on what that person already thinks. For example, you observe a person talking to himself and conclude that he has a mental illness…until he walks a little closer and you spot the Bluetooth headset hidden in his ear. Perception can enormously influence judgement.

For this reason, good researchers try to identify and address any pre-conceived notions that might affect – or even distort – what is being observed. This is particularly important when there is some attempt to generalize or standardize the observations collected by several researchers working on the same topic at the same time.

Participation

Source: Rawpixel.com/Shutterstock.com

Often, qualitative researchers engage in a particular type of observation called participant observation. This type of data collection involves the researcher entering and participating in the social setting that is under observation. Researching “beer-league” baseball? Join a team. Want to understand the values of Boy Scout families? Take your kid to Boy Scouts (and bring a notepad for your notes!).

Preservation

Source: XiXinXing/Shutterstock.com

Preserving the “naturalness” of the setting is often a priority for participant observers. For this reason, they sometimes elect to go undercover. This may require some additional justification and consideration when applying for Research Ethics Board approval.
