Conceptual Frameworks

Source: Christophe BOISSON/Shutterstock.com

Not everyone sees the world in the same way. Some people, for example, think that the use of technology by children impairs their healthy development. Others believe it is crucial skill-building in an increasingly wired world. Some people think that students learn best in lecture settings, while others believe that learning-by-doing is the best way to take in new information. Our ways of understanding the world our conceptual frameworks vary widely from person to person.

Our beliefs also change over time. It was once thought that the sun orbited the earth or that sickness was transmitted via bad smells. These frameworks have incorporated new discoveries and have evolved accordingly.

It is a fact that researchers ask different types questions depending on their personal framework (way of thinking). For example, sociologists sometimes differ in the way they believe power is organized in society. While conflict theorists, for example, tend to see power imbalances as related to class, feminist sociologists conceive of inequality deriving from patriarchy. The research approaches taken by these two different camps of sociologist generally reflect the significant differences in their way of understanding the world.

Literature reviews require that the researcher identify the conceptual frameworks underpinning the work he or she is reviewing. It is also common to conclude a literature review with a statement of the conceptual framework the researcher will use in his or her research: the way he or she understands the topic he or she is setting out to explore.

Here's an example of a conceptual framework identified in the research project Immigrants' Perceptions of Housing Discrimination in Toronto: The Housing New Canadians Project by Kenneth L. Dion Journal, published in 2001 in the Journal of Social Issues.

The Conceptual Framework

[This project's] conceptual framework views housing experiences of immigrants from a multilevel perspective. At the macro or societal level, each receiving country has defining institutional arrangements for incorporating newcomers, such as whether or not they provide immigrants and refugees with financial assistance for housing. Below that is the meso or group level, at which differential incorporation and access to resources by ethnic and immigrant groups takes place. Different factors may serve as barriers to incorporation of different immigrant groups at the meso level.
