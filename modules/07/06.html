Qualitative Research

Recall that with qualitative research, real effort is required during the data analysis stage.

Qualitative data typically comes in one of two forms: words (spoken or written) or images (observed and captured or created by a participant).

It is very common for a researcher to have collected a lot of words (or images) prior to the analysis phase of the research. That is why the data analysis in qualitative research can be particularly time consuming.

Qualitative research is often referred to as inductive, meaning that generalized statements or theories about a social setting are derived from the detailed analysis of localized data. That means that the researchers might study one small population in the hopes of understanding a little more about a larger population.

A researcher who wants to contribute to the pool of knowledge about athletic culture at Humber, for example, might choose to focus on just the varsity men’s rugby team; an in-depth study about just this group will potentially shed some light on the athletic culture in general at Humber College. (Because qualitative research tends to be researcher-centred, the researcher will be sure to consider how his or her view of athletics might impact the data!)

Let's assume that a researcher interviews the members of the varsity men’s rugby team. Each interview, which lasts roughly one hour, is recorded and transcribed. The researcher also joins the team for several weekend tournaments and carefully notes her observations. Finally, she is left with a stack of paper from which she is hoping to draw some conclusions.
