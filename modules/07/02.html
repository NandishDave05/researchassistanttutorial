Many students completing this online course will be – or are currently – engaged in a research project. You may be asked to help refine data collection tools by completing pilot surveys or working through interview questions. You may be asked to publically explain the project, help recruit participants, and secure their informed consent. You may even be asked to participate in observational data collection, counting or describing the behaviour you are observing.

Finally, some of you may even be asked to help analyse the data you collect. As Denscombe so succinctly puts it, “the purpose of analysing something is to gain a better understanding of it. Through a detailed examination of the thing that is being studied the aim is either to "describe the constituent elements, to explain how it works, or to interpret what it means" ” (2010). This Module will help you acquire the skills you will need to achieve those important goals.

Given that data is often collected using two disparate research approaches (quantitative or qualitative), we will start with one and then move on to the other. Do not forget though – your research project may include a “mixed method” approach, as researchers are increasingly discovering ways that these approaches scaffold each other quite nicely.

Let’s start with quantitative data analysis and some of the most common types that you might encounter in your work as a research assistant.
